Whether you do it when you’re awake or asleep, teeth grinding can lead to numerous dental problems later on. Known in the dental and medical community as bruxism, it’s an all-too-common condition that occurs in some 30-50 million Americans. Unfortunately, most people go throughout their daily lives completely oblivious to the negative health effects of teeth grinding. It’s not until the condition has created unwanted side effects that most people realize the true impact of grinding their teeth.

Causes of Teeth Grinding

Before we go into some of the problems associated with teeth grinding, let’s first discuss the causes of this condition. One of the most common causes of teeth grinding is stress (mental, not physical). It’s a common assumption that stress is only psychological and doesn’t manifest itself through physical symptoms; however, this simply isn’t true.

According to StatisticsBrain.com, 77% of people living in the U.S. report regular physical symptoms brought on by stress, including teeth grinding. When you’re stressed, the muscles in your body instinctively tighten. In turn, this forces the jaw shut, causing the individual to grind his or her teeth.

Other causes of teeth grinding include the temporormandibular joint (or TMJ, the jaw joint), insomnia, gum disease, and malocclusion (misalignment). Children are more likely to grind their teeth, but this condition may appear in people of all ages.

Problems Caused by Teeth Grinding

Headaches

You might be surprised to learn that grinding your teeth for long periods of time can trigger headaches. Constantly forcing pressure against your upper and lower jawbone stresses the nervous system, which may trigger a headache or migraine. It’s not uncommon for people to wake up with a headache and/or earache after grinding their teeth at night.

Damages Teeth

Of course, bruxism can also lead to tooth damage. The outer layer of enamel protecting our teeth is incredibly strong, but this doesn’t necessarily mean it will hold up under the stress of grinding. Grinding your teeth night after night may result in wearing of the enamel or even create cracks and fractures.

If you have any cavities, bridges or other dental work, grinding your teeth could leave them vulnerable to damage.

Underbite or Overbite

Teeth grinding increases the risk of developing an underbite or overbite. Whether you’re young, old or anywhere in between, your natural jaw position may shift due to bruxism. It’s not something that happens overnight, but several years of teeth grinding may lead to the development of either an underbite or overbite.

According to data collected by the American Dental Association, emergency rooms are seeing more and more dental emergencies.

Almost all age ranges showed a slight increase or fluctuation in the percentage of dental visits being for an emergency, but the rate was much higher among those between the ages of 21 and 34. In 2000, emergency visits accounted for 1.5 percent of all dental visits for that age group, and by 2010 that figure had doubled.

This leads the ADA and other experts to believe that insurance is the main cause for this increase. Young people have seen their dental coverage erode or disappear entirely as employers trim benefits and the government has not updated requirements.

Even the Affordable Care Act does not require dental insurance to be offered to adults (but there is a requirement for children). This means that people can’t go to the dentist for routine checkups, allowing small problems the chance to evolve into bigger ones. Then when the pain becomes unbearable, the patient is more likely to go to the ER rather than an emergency dentist.

As if they needed another reason, this gives dentists a chance to underline why routine visits and cleanings are so important. Small problems can either go unnoticed or untreated, in which case they can turn into a big problem. That means pain, discomfort, and a higher bill.

Besides, most of the time, there is nothing that can be done for a dental emergency at an emergency room. Your best bet is to see an emergency dentist.

Gum recession, or gingival recession, is a condition in which the gum surrounding the teeth begins to gradually pull back towards the jaw. As the gum tissue continues to recede, the sensitive portion of the tooth’s roots are left exposed and vulnerable to sensitivity, decay and bacteria. This is why it’s important for individuals to take a proactive approach towards preventing gum recession. In this post, we’re going to take a look at some of the most common causes of gum recession.

 

Cause #1) Aggressive Brushing

The California Dental Association (CDA) states, “gums that recede to expose the root surfaces of teeth is a common condition in adults over the age of 40. Many consider it to be just a sign of aging, and in some cases it is essentially that—often the result of wear and tear or years of aggressive tooth brushing.”

We’ve been taught at an early age that brushing is essential to preventing tooth decay, but a topic that’s oftentimes overlooked is aggressive brushing. When you press against your teeth and gums with too much force, it may cause the gums to recede. Be conscious of your brushing technique and if your gums feel sore or tender afterward, use less force the next time you brush.

 

Cause #2) Periodontal Disease

Another common cause of gum recession is periodontal disease (gum disease). It’s estimated that three out four adults have at least some form of periodontal disease. Drinking lots of sugar-rich, heavily acidic beverages may contribute to this disease, as does smoking cigarettes. If your lifestyle includes either of these bad habits, consider making some changes to improve the health of your gums (as well as your overall health).

 

Cause #3) Heredity

Unfortunately, some people are naturally predisposed to gum recession due to genetics. If your parents and grandparents experience this condition, there’s a good chance you will too. However, you can help protect your teeth and gums from the effects of gum recession by brushing lightly and flossing on a regular basis.

 

Cause #4) Trauma

A fourth possible cause of gum recession is direct physical trauma. For instance, hockey players are placed at a high risk for gum recession, along with other dental problems, due to the constant blows they receive to their mouth. Although a mouthpiece helps to absorb some of the blow, their teeth and gums are still vulnerable to the physical trauma of behind hit game after game.

If you’ve ever played (or are simply a fan of) contact sports like football, rugby, or ice hockey, you know that injuries are part of the game. Injuries to the teeth, mouth, and jaw are fairly common too. But no matter how tough the athlete, these dental emergencies are very painful and often require immediate attention to repair the damage.

As you can imagine, there is a huge variety of possible dental injuries that can result from a sports accident. From high-contact sports like boxing and hockey to low or no-contact sports like basketball or track and field, a cracked or knocked-out tooth is never out of the realm of possibility.

Very minor problems like a chipped or cracked tooth can usually be easily repaired by an emergency dentist, while more complicated problems like multiple knocked out teeth or damage to the jaw are more difficult to fix. Most problems can be corrected through cosmetic dentistry procedures like bonding, crowns, bridges, or implants. If you have a tooth knocked out, the original tooth can sometimes be replaced with quick and appropriate attention.

Preventing sports injuries can be at the same time simple and difficult. Something as simple as wearing a fitted mouth guard can prevent most dental injuries by protecting the teeth and keeping the mouth from moving during contact.

In most cases, helmets with face masks are a must in high-contact sports. The helmet lowers the chance of a head injury such as a concussion while the face mask prevents contact to the face and mouth by other players, the ground, or equipment. Two of the few exceptions are boxing and rugby, where a hard helmet and face mask would hurt other players during the match.

We would all love to have bright white teeth, but not all of us are so lucky. Plus, some of us may not realize it until it’s too late that there are many foods we eat that will stain our teeth. In most cases, tooth stains are preventable and can be drastically slowed by changing your habits or fixed by a dentist.

Some foods that stain your teeth are fairly obvious. Your teeth are white, after all, and what may stain a white shirt could also stain white teeth. Other foods, however, you may not have guessed have a staining effect. Let’s look at a few:

Coffee and Black Tea

You probably imagined that these would be at the top of the list. So many Americans drink one or both on a daily basis that many of us have stained teeth due to this habit. Both contain chromagen, which is believed to be a powerful tooth stainer.

Red Wine

If you’ve ever had to try to get a red wine stain out of your clothes, your carpet, or your white counter top, you have an idea about how aggressively red wine stains. Red wine contains chromagen, tannins, and acid, which all act to stain teeth along with the deep red color.

Sports Drinks and Soda

Even if they don’t contain sugar, both kinds of drinks are highly acidic which not only contribute to staining teeth, but also to decay and cavities.

Spices and Dark Sauces

Many contain aggressive colors and are acidic, meaning they can be powerful stainers if used on a regular basis. Think of balsamic vinegar: few other foods look so much like ink, and it stains the same way!

Sweets

In addition to the sugar attacking on your teeth, so can colored candies. If you notice your tongue changing color, chances are those colorings are having an effect on your teeth too.

Even if some sources of tooth stains are difficult to avoid, there is good news. With good preventative hygiene and modern whitening procedures, you can still have a bright white smile! The principal angle of sugar-free chewing gum advertising has always been that it’s good for your teeth. However, recent studies by dentists and researchers have provided some evidence to suggest that this may not be true.

At first glance, it may seem like advertisers have been lying to us for years. In fact, xylitol, the sugar substitute normally used in sugar-free gum, has been called “tooth friendly” by both the American Dental Association and the European Union. So what gives?

When you look closer at the facts of the study, it becomes apparent that the culprit for tooth decay isn’t the sugar substitute or the gum itself, but the acidic flavorings added to the gum.

As dentists know, it’s acid that decays teeth, not just sugar. Sugar leads to the formation of plaque on the surface of the teeth, which attracts bacteria, which results in acid. The acid is what causes decay, so of course, an acidic flavoring in a sugar-free gum would counteract the benefit of not containing sugar.

However, sugar-free gum with non-acidic flavorings are still considered to be OK by the ADA. Here’s what they have to say:

“[Sugar-free gums] are sweetened by non-cavity causing sweeteners such as aspartame, sorbitol or mannitol.  Chewing sugarless gum has been shown to increase the flow of saliva, thereby reducing plaque acid, strengthening the teeth and reducing tooth decay.  Of course, chewing sugar-containing gum also increases saliva flow, but it also contains sugar which is used by plaque bacteria to produce decay-causing acids. Further research needs to be done to determine the effects of chewing sugar-containing gum on tooth decay.”

You can find a list of ADA accepted products here, or you can ask your dentist for his professional opinion.

For dentists, treating elderly patients can be a challenge. In fact, it’s often just as much of a challenge for these elderly patients to keep up with their own dental hygiene. As people get older, their cognitive and physical abilities begin to weaken. Oral health may not be as easy to keep up with anymore, and it may not be as much of a personal priority as it used to.

These problems are practically inevitable, so it’s often in a dentist’s—and a patient’s—best interest to attempt to prepare for these eventualities long before they become problems. Steps that can be taken for 50 to 60-year-old patients include education about proper care, establishing a plan for future dental visits, and creating a financial plan for the future.

This last issue is a very significant one for many elderly patients as public health programs including Medicare often do not include even basic dental health coverage. Even with expansions under programs such as the Affordable Care Act (also known as Obamacare), it’s estimated that only two percent of elderly Americans will have oral health coverage of any kind.

This problem adds to complications concerning cosmetic dentistry procedures, such as dentures or dental implants. While these procedures can certainly help someone with missing or very badly decayed teeth with their dental function, they often must be paid for out-of-pocket.

That said, the primary focus for elderly dental care is function, not aesthetics. While a great-looking smile is prized at any age, the focus for elderly patients should be placed more on preventing infection and decay rather than cosmetic dentistry. There is more and more evidence that poor oral health can contribute to poor overall health, including an increased risk of heart attack and stroke.

Often, we don’t give our ancient ancestors enough credit.

When we look at achievements like the Pyramids at Giza, the huge, advanced societies of the ancient far East, or the astonishing engineering feats of the Romans, we wonder how they could have possibly done it without modern tools and knowledge. However, humans have always been intensely curious and resourceful, so these accomplishments shouldn’t be much of a surprise.

Many of us have similar ideas on a much smaller scale. For example, would you believe that there have been skilled and knowledgeable dentists for 2,500 years? What about for 9,000 years? In fact, both are true! Working with drill bits tipped with hard stones like flint and obsidian, these ancient dentists not only attempted to fix dental problems, but also performed purely cosmetic dentistry procedures.

For example, in this article in National Geographic, the author points out that the ancient equivalent of “grills” were an extremely popular cosmetic dentistry procedure in Central America long before Nelly came to St. Louis and started singing about them. In the picture provided of a recovered skull, you can clearly see stones fitted into the teeth of this person, in addition to some grooves which had been filed out purely for cosmetic purposes.

While this example of 2,500 year-old cosmetic dentistry is impressive in itself, the researchers involved with the dig also have evidence to determine that the dentists also had enough knowledge to know how far to drill into the tooth as not to reach the sensitive pulp, cause a dangerous infection, or risk cracking the tooth.

We can only guess this knowledge was gained through extremely painful trial and error. Luckily, technology has improved in this area as well! Additionally, there are of course many cosmetic dentistry options these days designed to make your teeth look more naturally healthy, such as whitening, veneers, bonding, Invisalign, and more.

You may not think an oral infection can kill, but they can and do. In fact, a recent article in the New York Times and a study from the Journal of Endodontics report that the hospitalization and morbidity rates of oral infections are on the rise.

The study was conducted by researchers in Boston and tracked cases of dental abscesses from 2000 to 2008. The data showed that hospitalization due to dental abscesses increased by 40% during those years, from 5,757 in 2000 to 8,141 in 2008, with 66 of those cases resulting in death.

Such a significant jump is alarming to many dentists and oral health professionals. This study should also send a powerful signal to patients as well, since most oral infections are the result of tooth decay and/or periodontal (gum) disease, which is usually the result of a patient’s neglect of good oral hygiene or of timely treatment.

An abscess can occur in on any part of the body, and is a natural response to infection. Oral abscesses can be especially painful and dangerous because the tissue in the mouth is sensitive and because swelling resulting from fluid collecting in the abscess could possibly restrict the airway, making it difficult or impossible to breathe.

That is why it’s so important to get early treatment for any kind of tooth pain, especially an infection. Fluid from the swelling that accumulates around an infection can spread to other parts of the mouth and jaw, and even lymph glands in the neck can react to an infection in the mouth.

The researchers who conducted the study along with the dental professionals cited in the article agree that the best form of treatment when it comes to oral health problems like abscesses and infections is prevention. In 2009, it was estimated that over 800,000 emergency room visits were the result of preventable dental conditions, and that during the period of the above study, these preventable conditions cost $858.9 million to treat.

Make sure you schedule regular cleanings and checkups with your dentist in order to prevent problems like these. Taking steps to properly care for your oral health now could save lots of pain, complications, and money down the road.

Tooth whitening is a cosmetic dentistry procedure that does exactly what it sounds like: whitens teeth. But to start, a better question may be: how does teeth staining work?

The tooth is made up of several layers of different materials. There’s the very inside of the tooth, called the pulp, which is the soft connective tissue that contains all of the nerve endings and blood vessels in the tooth. Surrounding the pulp above the gum is dentin, a harder, yellow-colored material that immediately protects the pulp.

(click for full size)

Finally, surrounding the dentin is the enamel, the white part of the tooth that we see. The enamel is the first line of defense for the tooth, and is made up of the hardest material in the human body. When we eat or drink–or simply exist–the enamel takes the punishment from discoloration to bacteria growth. These tiny layers of discoloring particles build up on top of the enamel, which is why brushing, flossing, and other good dental hygiene practices are so important!

Still, enamel is porous on a microscopic level, so discoloration from things like coffee and smoking can eventually ‘sink into’ the tooth, making removing these stains with a toothbrush nearly impossible.

What then? You may be a candidate for tooth whitening. Professional tooth whitening uses controlled application of different chemicals, mainly hydrogen peroxide. When applied to the teeth in the form of a gel or a paste and then rinsed off, these chemicals gently get into the pores of the enamel and safely brighten up those stains, leaving you with a shining white smile!

By choosing to whiten your teeth with a cosmetic dentist, you will see significant results after only one whitening session!
